# The Individualistic Fallacy, Ecological Studies and Instrumental Variables

**Full citation:** Loney, T., Nagelkerke, N. (2014). The individualistic fallacy, ecological studies and instrumental variables: a causal interpretation. *Emerging Themes in Epidemiology*, 11, 18.

**DOI:** [10.1186/1742-7622-11-18](https://doi.org/10.1186/1742-7622-11-18)

**Type:** Journal article

**Bib key:** Loney2014

## Abstract

Examines the relationship between ecological studies, the ecological fallacy, and instrumental variable (IV) approaches to causal inference. Argues that ecological studies, despite the well-known ecological fallacy, can provide valid causal inference when aggregate exposures serve as instrumental variables meeting the conditions of monotonicity and independence from confounders.

## Key Findings

- Ecological fallacy: bias that arises when associations observed at the aggregate (population) level differ from associations at the individual level.
- The "individualistic fallacy" is the converse: assuming that individual-level associations hold at the population level.
- Argues that ecological studies can be valuable when aggregate-level exposures act as instrumental variables.
- Conditions for valid IV interpretation: monotonicity (the aggregate exposure affects individuals in only one direction) and independence from unmeasured confounders.
- Examples where ecological data provide useful causal evidence: air pollution time-series studies, bicycle helmet legislation, public health policy evaluations.
- Ecological study designs remain useful for policy evaluation when individual-level data are unavailable or infeasible.

## Key Data Points

- Ecological fallacy: aggregate associations =/= individual-level associations
- IV conditions: monotonicity, independence from confounders
- Examples: air pollution time series, helmet laws
