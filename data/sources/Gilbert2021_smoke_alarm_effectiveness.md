# Estimating Smoke Alarm Effectiveness in Homes

**Full citation:** Gilbert, S.W. (2021). Estimating Smoke Alarm Effectiveness in Homes. *Fire Technology*, 57, 1497-1516.

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10694-020-01072-z](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10694-020-01072-z)

**Type:** Journal article

**Bib key:** Gilbert2021

## Abstract

Develops a fire risk model to estimate the effectiveness of smoke alarms in residential settings using NFIRS (National Fire Incident Reporting System) data. The model incorporates probabilities of ignition, detection, reporting, and casualties to estimate the reduction in fires reported and casualties attributable to smoke alarm presence.

## Key Findings

- Smoke alarms reduce the expected number of fires reported from a non-smoke-alarm residence by a factor of 3.5 to 5 (approximately 70-80% reduction in reported fires).
- Smoke alarms reduce expected casualties by a factor of 2.5 to 3.5 (approximately 60-70% reduction in casualties).
- Uses NFIRS data and a probabilistic fire risk model that accounts for ignition, detection, reporting, and casualty probabilities.
- Actual smoke alarm usage in the US is probably below the commonly reported 92% figure.
- The effectiveness estimate depends on the assumed fraction of homes with working smoke alarms.
- Working smoke alarms primarily improve outcomes through earlier detection and faster occupant response.

## Key Data Points

- Casualty reduction factor: 2.5 to 3.5 (60-70% reduction)
- Fire reporting reduction factor: 3.5 to 5 (70-80% reduction)
- Based on NFIRS data and probabilistic risk modeling
- Actual smoke alarm prevalence likely below 92%
