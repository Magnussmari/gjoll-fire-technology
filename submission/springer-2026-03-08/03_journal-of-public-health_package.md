## Tailored title
Fatal Fire Incidents in Iceland, 1968–2025: A Population-Level Injury Prevention Analysis of Seasonality, Housing Age, and Regulation

## Tailored abstract
Background: Long-term incident-level evidence for fatal fires is limited in small populations, reducing policy precision for prevention. Evaluating whether fire safety regulations translate into measurable public health outcomes requires both statistical evidence and contextual understanding of institutional effectiveness.

Methods: We analyzed a national Icelandic registry of all fatal fire incidents (1968–2025), with cross-source verification and population denominators. We examined period trends, seasonal concentration, structure versus non-structure fatalities, building construction-year patterns, and segmented time-series change around regulatory transition. The lead author's decade of operational experience as a firefighter and emergency medical technician informed interpretation of statistical patterns.

Results: The registry contains 113 incidents and 145 deaths. Structure fires represent 80% of deaths. Fatal burden peaks in the 1970s and declines substantially in later decades. Incidents and deaths cluster in the October–March heating season (58%). Among structure-fire incidents with known construction year, no fatal incidents occurred in post-1998 buildings; missing construction-year records are confined to earlier decades. Person-years approximation yields a marked pre/post contrast (0.57 vs 0.00 per 100,000). Interrupted time-series models show a strong pre-existing decline and a significant level decrease near the 1999 policy breakpoint, with expected uncertainty under rare-event robustness specifications.

Conclusions: Fatal fire mortality in Iceland has declined markedly but remains socially and seasonally patterned. Results are consistent with cumulative public-health benefit from regulation and safer housing standards, while indicating residual preventable risk in older building stock and vulnerable occupancy contexts. The documented pattern of institutional restructuring and unfunded government action plans raises questions about the sustainability of these gains. Prevention strategy should prioritize winter-risk communication, legacy-housing retrofit incentives, and cross-sector surveillance.

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

Please consider our manuscript, "Fatal Fire Incidents in Iceland, 1968–2025: A Population-Level Injury Prevention Analysis of Seasonality, Housing Age, and Regulation," for publication in the Journal of Public Health.

This manuscript provides a long-term national injury epidemiology analysis of fire mortality using incident-level data linked to population denominators. It integrates descriptive epidemiology, seasonal risk analysis, housing-age stratification, and interrupted time-series modeling to evaluate population-level change and prevention-relevant patterns. The analysis is enriched by a practitioner perspective and an institutional effectiveness framework that examines whether documented gains are sustainable.

The main contribution is practical public-health evidence: declining long-term mortality with persistent concentration in heating-season months and older housing contexts, plus policy-relevant interpretation for prevention targeting, retrofit incentives, and intersectoral governance.

The paper is original, not under review elsewhere, and approved by all authors. Data and reproducibility materials are publicly available (DOI: 10.34881/I5WGJU).
