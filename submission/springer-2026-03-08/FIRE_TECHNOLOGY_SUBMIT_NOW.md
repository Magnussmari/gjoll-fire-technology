### STEP 5: Abstract

Copy-paste (225 words):

```
Fire-related fatalities are rare events requiring long observation windows for meaningful trend analysis. This study presents a 58-year national dataset of all fatal fire incidents in Iceland (1968–2025), compiled through systematic historical source review with cross-verification. The registry contains 113 fatal incidents resulting in 145 deaths; structure fires account for 80%. Fatal incidents cluster in the October–March heating season (58%). Decade-aggregated mortality peaked in the 1970s (21.9 deaths per million per year) and declined to approximately 4–7 per million from the 1990s onward. Construction year is complete from 1996 onward; in this complete subset, no fatal incidents occurred in buildings constructed after 1998—the year Iceland adopted modern prescriptive fire safety requirements including mandatory smoke detectors and extinguishers in new dwellings. A person-years exposure approximation yields 0.57 per 100,000 in pre-1998 dwellings versus 0.00 in post-1998 dwellings. Interrupted time series analysis identifies a significant pre-existing declining trend and a level decrease at the 1999 regulatory intervention, though a negative binomial robustness check yields wider confidence intervals reflecting limited rare-event power. Framing findings through institutional effectiveness analysis, the study examines whether gains are sustainable given repeated organizational restructuring and unfunded action plans. Results are consistent with cumulative effectiveness of Iceland's fire safety framework but cannot separate regulatory effects from concurrent technological and sociodemographic changes. Persistent seasonal risk and concentration of fatalities in older building stock are documented.
```

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

Please consider our manuscript, "Fatal Fire Incidents in Iceland, 1968–2025: Long-Term Trends, Building Age, and the Effectiveness of Fire Safety Regulation," for publication in Fire Technology.

This study presents a national 58-year incident-level dataset on fatal fire incidents in Iceland, combining descriptive fire epidemiology, construction-year stratification, person-years rate approximation, and interrupted time series modeling. The core empirical result is that in the period with complete construction-year recording, no fatal structure-fire incidents occurred in post-1998 buildings, alongside a sustained long-term mortality decline and persistent seasonal concentration during the heating period.

The manuscript goes beyond statistical description to examine the institutional effectiveness of Iceland's fire safety regulatory framework, drawing on the governance literature to assess whether the documented gains are sustainable. A new section on "paper versus practice" addresses the gap between regulatory outputs and measurable outcomes, informed by the lead author's decade of operational experience as a firefighter and emergency medical technician.

We believe the manuscript is a strong fit for Fire Technology because it addresses fire safety in the built environment, links outcomes to building and regulatory context, and contributes to evidence-based fire safety policy evaluation.
