## Tailored title
Fatal Fire Incidents in Iceland, 1968–2025: Long-Term Trends, Building Age, and the Effectiveness of Fire Safety Regulation

## Tailored abstract
Fire-related fatalities are rare events that require long observation windows for meaningful trend analysis. This study presents a 58-year national dataset of all fatal fire incidents in Iceland (1968–2025), compiled through systematic historical source review with cross-verification across independent sources. The Gjöll incident registry contains 113 fatal incidents resulting in 145 deaths. Structure fires account for 93 incidents (116 deaths, 80%), while other fire-related incidents (maritime and vehicle) account for 20 incidents (29 deaths, 20%). Fatal incidents cluster in the October–March heating season (58% of incidents and deaths). Decade-aggregated mortality peaked in the 1970s and declined substantially to approximately 4–7 deaths per million from the 1990s onward. Among incidents with known construction year, no fatal structure-fire incidents occurred in buildings constructed after 1998—the year Iceland adopted modern prescriptive fire safety requirements. A person-years exposure approximation yields a fatality rate of 0.57 per 100,000 person-years in pre-1998 dwellings versus 0.00 in post-1998 dwellings. Interrupted time series analysis identifies a significant pre-existing declining trend and a level decrease at the 1999 regulatory intervention. Framing these findings through the lens of institutional effectiveness, the study also examines whether the regulatory framework's gains are sustainable given repeated institutional restructuring and unfunded action plans. Persistent seasonal risk and emerging challenges including unauthorized residential use of commercial buildings and arson targeting vulnerable populations are documented.

## Cover letter (copy-paste)
Dear Editor-in-Chief,

Please consider our manuscript, "Fatal Fire Incidents in Iceland, 1968–2025: Long-Term Trends, Building Age, and the Effectiveness of Fire Safety Regulation," for publication in Fire Technology.

This study presents a national 58-year incident-level dataset on fatal fire incidents in Iceland, combining descriptive fire epidemiology, construction-year stratification, person-years rate approximation, and interrupted time series modeling. The core empirical result is that in the period with complete construction-year recording, no fatal structure-fire incidents occurred in post-1998 buildings, alongside a sustained long-term mortality decline and persistent seasonal concentration during the heating period.

The manuscript goes beyond statistical description to examine the institutional effectiveness of Iceland's fire safety regulatory framework, drawing on the governance literature to assess whether the documented gains are sustainable. A new section on "paper versus practice" addresses the gap between regulatory outputs and measurable outcomes, informed by the lead author's decade of operational experience as a firefighter and emergency medical technician.

We believe the manuscript is a strong fit for Fire Technology because it addresses fire safety in the built environment, links outcomes to building and regulatory context, and contributes to evidence-based fire safety policy evaluation.

The manuscript is original, not under consideration elsewhere, and approved by all authors. Data and reproducibility materials are publicly available (DOI: 10.34881/I5WGJU).
