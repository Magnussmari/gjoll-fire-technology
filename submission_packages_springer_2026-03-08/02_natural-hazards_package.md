## Tailored title
Fatal Fire Mortality as a Long-Term Urban Hazard in Iceland (1968–2025): Seasonality, Building Stock Age, and Regulatory Effectiveness

## Tailored abstract
This article analyzes fatal fire mortality in Iceland as a long-term socio-technical hazard using a complete national incident registry from 1968–2025. The dataset includes 113 fatal incidents and 145 deaths, with 93 structure-fire incidents (116 deaths) and 20 non-structure incidents (29 deaths). Hazard burden is strongly seasonal: 58% of incidents and deaths occur during the October–March heating season. Decade-based rates show substantial long-term decline after a 1970s peak, with low but persistent residual mortality in recent decades. Building-stock analysis indicates concentration of fatal structure fires in older buildings; in the subset with complete construction-year coverage, no fatal structure-fire incidents occur in post-1998 buildings. Exposure approximation and exact Poisson bounds indicate this zero-event observation is informative rather than purely denominator-driven. Segmented Poisson time-series modeling identifies a significant pre-existing decline and a marked level decrease at the 1999 policy breakpoint, while robustness models show wider uncertainty expected for rare events. Framing these findings through institutional effectiveness analysis, the study also examines whether repeated organizational restructuring and unfunded government action plans may threaten the sustainability of documented safety gains. The findings support interpretation of residential fire mortality as a preventable urban hazard shaped by infrastructure age, seasonal exposure, regulatory implementation, and institutional capacity.

## Keywords

## Cover letter (copy-paste)
Dear Editors,

We submit the manuscript "Fatal Fire Mortality as a Long-Term Urban Hazard in Iceland (1968–2025): Seasonality, Building Stock Age, and Regulatory Effectiveness" for consideration in Natural Hazards.

The paper presents a national longitudinal hazard dataset and examines fire mortality through risk assessment and mitigation lenses. We quantify long-term change, heating-season concentration, and building-age vulnerability, and apply interrupted time series analysis around a major regulatory transition. The analysis extends beyond statistical description to evaluate the institutional effectiveness of Iceland's fire safety framework, including the sustainability of documented gains under conditions of organizational restructuring.

The manuscript aligns with Natural Hazards by combining empirical hazard characterization, risk-relevant stratification, and mitigation-oriented interpretation. It offers transferable lessons on how regulatory and infrastructural transitions affect rare but high-consequence mortality outcomes in cold-climate urban systems.

This manuscript is original, not under review elsewhere, and approved by all authors. Data and reproducibility materials are publicly available (DOI: 10.34881/I5WGJU).
